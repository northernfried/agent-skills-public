Product Revenue Tier & Investment Case

A working model for deciding whether a product or solution deserves investment — classify its revenue contribution, place it on the value hierarchy if it's direct revenue, then run it through the investment gate before committing.

01 / Classification

Direct or indirect revenue contributor?

Every product/solution starts here. This single fork decides which model applies next — the value hierarchy for direct revenue, or a quantified enablement case for indirect revenue.

Direct revenue

The client pays for this, specifically

A line item, a subscription, a metered fee — money changes hands because of this offering, not because of something it happens to sit beside.

→ Continue to the Value Hierarchy below

Indirect revenue

It enables revenue elsewhere

No standalone client charge — value shows up as speed, cost, or margin somewhere else in the business. Click to see the four named mechanisms.

03 / Investment Gate

Does the case clear the bar?

Answer the two questions below in order. There are exactly three valid outcomes — a quantitative direct case, a quantitative indirect case, or a labeled competitive-parity exception. There is no fourth "trust me" outcome.

Q1 — Is there a quantitatively supported revenue case (direct tier placement or indirect mechanism), sourced and verifiable?
Q2 — Without a quantitative case: does this close a competitive messaging/parity gap (a defensive "we lose deals without it" case)?
04 / Evidence Discipline

Quantitative decides. Qualitative confirms.

Quantitative — the case itself

  • Revenue, margin, volume, cost, and cycle-time figures
  • Sourced — internal system-of-record or named competitor/market data
  • Tagged by access tier: public, gated, paid, or internal
  • Unverified figures labeled "estimate needing validation" — never stated as fact

Qualitative — confirmation only

  • Client interviews, NPS/CSAT commentary, sales anecdotes
  • Used to confirm value already shown quantitatively
  • Appropriate for client experience and differentiation topics
  • Never a substitute for a revenue or cost estimate — runs systematically optimistic
05 / Offering Types

Product, Service, or Solution?

Identify this before placing anything on the matrix below — the same underlying capability can exist as more than one type, and each instance is placed separately.

Product

Standardized and repeatable. The same thing is sold and delivered to every client with minimal customization. Scales without proportional headcount.

Service

Expertise or labor, delivered ongoing or per-engagement. Varies by client, people-intensive, billed by time, retainer, or outcome.

Solution

A bundle of product(s) and/or service(s) solving one named client problem end-to-end. The client buys the outcome, not the parts.

07 / The Diagonal

How drift actually plays out

Introduction/Transformational → Growth/Strategic → Maturity/Efficiency → Decline/Foundational is the default path for a successful offering nobody reinvests in. Differentiation erodes over time — an offering that stands still on the tier axis moves down it anyway as competitors close the gap, while it simultaneously moves right along the lifecycle axis. Reinvestment pulls it back up a tier, which effectively starts a new diagonal rather than undoing the lifecycle clock.
